For most of my career, data was something I worked *with*, not something I studied. As a purchasing and sales manager, I tracked supplier pricing, monitored margins, resolved discrepancies, and managed budgets that ran into the hundreds of thousands of dollars annually. Every decision I made was driven by numbers — but I was making sense of them manually, through spreadsheets, gut instinct, and experience.

Then I started asking a different question: *What if I could do this better?*

## The Moment It Changed

Working at Hub Garage Co., I handled 15 to 20 inbound leads every day. I knew which customers tended to close, which products had the highest margin, and which suppliers were worth negotiating harder with. But all of that knowledge lived in my head, or at best in a scattered Excel file.

I remember a moment during a busy week when I thought: *if I could visualize the last 90 days of sales data properly, I would know exactly where to focus my energy this week.* I didn't have the tools or the training to build that — yet. That moment planted the seed.

## Why Data Analytics, Specifically

I did not come to data analytics because it sounded trendy. I came to it because the problems I was already solving in my career — cost reduction, supplier performance, revenue forecasting, customer behavior — are exactly the kinds of problems that data analytics is built to address.

The field gave a formal name to skills I had been using informally: variance analysis, trend identification, segmentation, reporting. Learning SQL, Python, and Tableau has not felt like starting over. It has felt like building a sharper version of the same instincts I already had.

That said, the technical side is genuinely humbling. Writing a SQL query that actually returns what you intended is satisfying in a way that closing a deal never quite is. There is an exactness to it.

## What the Transition Actually Looks Like

I am currently enrolled in the Data Analytics program at triOS College in Toronto, and I will be honest: balancing coursework with everything else in life is not easy. The material demands real focus. Python is not something you can skim.

What has helped me most is anchoring every new concept to a real problem I have seen in my career. When I learned about RFM segmentation (Recency, Frequency, Monetary analysis), I immediately thought about the contractor accounts I managed at Hub Garage — how some customers bought frequently in small amounts, while others made large purchases once a quarter. RFM gave me a framework to think about that more precisely.

When I practiced SQL joins, I thought about reconciling purchase orders against delivery records in SAP. The logic was the same. The syntax was just new.

## Advice for Anyone Considering a Similar Move

If you are working in operations, finance, supply chain, or sales and thinking about moving into data analytics, here is what I would tell you:

**Your experience is not a detour — it is an asset.** Analysts who understand how businesses actually work are rare. Most people learning Python have never managed a supplier relationship or defended a pricing decision to a client. That context makes you better at asking the right questions of your data.

**Start with the tools you already use.** Advanced Excel was my entry point. Pivot Tables, VLOOKUP, conditional formatting — these are legitimate analytical tools. Once you are comfortable with them, SQL and Python feel like natural extensions, not a leap into the unknown.

**Be patient with yourself on the technical side.** There will be syntax errors, broken formulas, and moments where nothing works and you do not know why. That frustration is normal. Push through it.

## Where I Am Headed

My goal is to work in a business intelligence or data analytics role that connects operational data to real decisions — ideally in supply chain, distribution, or sales operations, where my background gives me an edge. I want to build the kind of dashboards and reports that let decision-makers act quickly and confidently.

I am still in the middle of this transition. The program does not finish until 2027. But I am already thinking differently about problems, and that shift started well before I enrolled.

If you are in a similar place — experienced in your field but hungry for something more systematic — I hope this was useful. Feel free to connect on LinkedIn or reach out by email. I am always glad to talk.
